The 8 Designated ClassificationsTokutei Meishoshu
What is itJapan's official premium sake categories. If a sake has one of these names on the label, it meets strict quality standards. These are the ones worth knowing.

Junmai 純米
MeaningPure rice — made from only rice, water, koji, and yeast. No added alcohol. No RPR minimum.
RPRNo legal minimum — typically 70% or below (30%+ polished away).
FlavourFull-bodied, rich, savoury, umami-forward. Earthy and robust. Pairs beautifully with food.
ServeRoom temp (20°C) or warm (40–45°C). Very versatile with temperature.
Pairs with
Grilled fishYakitoriRamenAged cheese
💬 How to sell it: "This is pure rice sake — nothing added, just rice and water. It has a rich, full flavour that goes really well with food. Think of it like a full-bodied white wine — great with savoury dishes."
Junmai Ginjo 純米吟醸
MeaningPure rice + ginjo-level polish. No added alcohol. Rice polished to at least 60% remaining (40% removed).
RPR60% or below.
FlavourFruity, floral, clean. Light to medium body. Apple, pear, melon, gentle rice. More aromatic than Junmai.
ServeChilled (10–15°C). Preserve the delicate aromas.
Pairs with
SashimiLight seafoodTofu dishesMild cheese
💬 How to sell it: "This is a great starter sake — it's fruity and floral, almost like a light white wine. Serve it cold and it's really refreshing. No sake flavour they might be worried about, just clean and delicate."
Junmai Daiginjo 純米大吟醸
MeaningPure rice + highest polish level. No added alcohol. The pinnacle of the Junmai category. Rice polished to at least 50% remaining.
RPR50% or below — half the rice grain polished away. Labour and ingredient intensive.
FlavourHighly aromatic, complex, elegant. Tropical fruit, white flowers, melon, clean finish. Very refined.
ServeWell chilled (8–12°C). Glencairn or white wine glass to capture aroma.
Pairs with
OystersSashimiLight sushiOn its own
💬 How to sell it: "This is premium sake — the rice has been polished down to almost half its original size, which is why it's so clean and aromatic. If they've never liked sake before, this is the one to change their mind. Serve it cold like a fine white wine."
Honjozo 本醸造
MeaningTrue brewed. A small amount of distilled alcohol added — not to increase volume, but to enhance aroma and lighten body. RPR 70% or below.
RPR70% or below.
FlavourLight, clean, slightly dry. Mild rice flavour, gentle aroma. Smooth and easy-drinking.
ServeChilled, room temp, or warm. Very versatile — great everyday sake.
Pairs with
TempuraGrilled chickenNoodlesEveryday Japanese
💬 How to sell it: "This is an everyday drinking sake — light, clean, and very food-friendly. The small amount of added alcohol actually makes it smoother and more aromatic, not stronger. Think of it as a lighter, crisper style."
Ginjo 吟醸
MeaningGinjo-level polish + small amount of added alcohol. Fruity and aromatic. More affordable entry to premium sake.
RPR60% or below.
FlavourFruity, floral, light. Apple, pear, banana, floral notes. Clean and aromatic. Similar to Junmai Ginjo but lighter.
ServeChilled (10–14°C).
Pairs with
SashimiSteamed seafoodLight salads
💬 How to sell it: "This is a fruity, fragrant sake — serve it cold and it almost smells like flowers and green apple. A great choice for someone who enjoys crisp white wine and wants to try something different."
Daiginjo 大吟醸
MeaningHighest polish + small added alcohol. The most refined and aromatic premium style. Highly polished, slow cold fermentation.
RPR50% or below.
FlavourIntensely aromatic, silky, ultra-refined. Tropical fruit, melon, white flowers, clean long finish.
ServeWell chilled (8–10°C). Wine glass to appreciate aromatics.
Pairs with
Delicate sashimiChawanmushiOn its own
💬 How to sell it: "This is competition-level sake — beautifully aromatic and silky smooth. If someone wants to impress at a dinner or try something truly special, this is the one. Serve it cold in a wine glass and just enjoy the aroma before tasting."
Tokubetsu Junmai 特別純米
MeaningSpecial Junmai. Pure rice sake with either higher polish than standard Junmai (60% or below) OR a special brewing technique. Brewer's discretion.
RPR60% or below, OR special brewing method declared on label.
FlavourVaries by producer. Generally between Junmai and Junmai Ginjo — more character than standard Junmai.
ServeChilled or room temp depending on style.
💬 How to sell it: "The 'tokubetsu' means special — the brewer has gone beyond the minimum requirements, either by polishing more or using a unique technique. It's a step up from regular Junmai with more complexity."
Tokubetsu Honjozo 特別本醸造
MeaningSpecial Honjozo. Like Honjozo but with higher polish (60% or below) OR a special brewing technique. Small added alcohol.
RPR60% or below, OR special method declared.
FlavourLighter and more aromatic than standard Honjozo. Clean, dry, subtle fruitiness. Easy drinking.
ServeChilled or slightly warm. Very food-friendly.
💬 How to sell it: "This is a step-up everyday sake — cleaner and more aromatic than standard, but still very approachable. Great value for the quality. Works warm or cold which makes it very flexible."
Quick ruleJunmai = pure rice only · No Junmai = small added alcohol · Ginjo = 60% RPR · Daiginjo = 50% RPR · Tokubetsu = special
Important Style TermsTerminology
What is itThese words appear on sake labels alongside the classification. Each changes the flavour, texture, or character significantly.
Tap to learn
Nama Nigori Genshu Muroka Kimoto / Yamahai Koshu Sparkling Taruzake
Nama 生 — Unpasteurised
MeaningSake that has not been pasteurised. Most sake is heated twice to stabilise it. Nama skips this entirely or partially.
FlavourFresh, lively, vibrant. Has a characteristic "namazake" freshness — like the difference between fresh bread and packaged bread. Slightly grassy, bright, energetic.
StorageMust be refrigerated at all times. Degrades quickly if not kept cold. Shorter shelf life.
Staff tipAlways keep in the cold section. Tell customers to refrigerate at home and drink within a few months of purchase.
💬 How to sell it: "Nama means it's unpasteurised — think of it like fresh juice versus packaged juice. It has this amazing fresh, vibrant quality you can't get in regular sake. Just keep it cold and enjoy it soon after opening."
Nigori 濁り — Cloudy Sake
MeaningRoughly filtered sake — rice solids left in. Ranges from lightly cloudy to very thick and milky.
FlavourCreamy, sweet, rich. Rice pudding, coconut, banana, vanilla. The residual rice adds sweetness and body.
ServeShake gently before pouring to mix the sediment. Serve chilled.
Staff tipMost popular sake style for first-timers — the sweetness and creaminess is very approachable.
💬 How to sell it: "Nigori is cloudy sake — it still has some rice in it which makes it creamy and a little sweet. It's actually the most popular style for people trying sake for the first time because it tastes almost like a light rice dessert. Serve it cold."
Genshu 原酒 — Undiluted
MeaningSake that has not been diluted with water after brewing. Most sake is diluted to ~15%. Genshu is typically 17–20% ABV.
FlavourBolder, richer, more concentrated. Intense flavour, more alcohol warmth. Still balanced — not harsh.
ServeNeat, on the rocks (the ice dilutes it slightly), or with a splash of water.
💬 How to sell it: "Genshu means undiluted — it comes straight from the tank without adding water, so it's a bit stronger and more concentrated. Think of it like cask-strength whisky. Great on the rocks where the ice slowly opens it up."
Muroka 無濾過 — Unfiltered
MeaningSake that has not been charcoal filtered. Most sake passes through activated charcoal to clarify and remove flavour compounds. Muroka skips this.
FlavourMore complex, raw, expressive. Can be slightly yellow/green in colour. More umami, more texture, more character.
NoteOften seen combined: Muroka Nama Genshu = unfiltered, unpasteurised, undiluted — the most natural expression possible.
💬 How to sell it: "Muroka means they didn't filter it through charcoal, so all the natural flavour and complexity is preserved. It can look slightly golden — that's normal. It tastes more bold and textured than regular sake."
Kimoto 生酛 / Yamahai 山廃
MeaningTraditional yeast starter methods. Kimoto = labour-intensive process using wooden poles to mash rice. Yamahai = simplified kimoto (no pole-ramming). Both allow wild lactic acid bacteria — more complex fermentation.
FlavourRicher, more complex, gamey, earthy, lactic. Yoghurt, earth, mushroom, umami. Robust and savoury. The opposite of delicate Ginjo styles.
ServeRoom temp or warm to bring out the complexity. Excellent with rich food.
💬 How to sell it: "Kimoto and Yamahai are traditional brewing methods — think of them like natural wine or sourdough. They use wild bacteria to develop more complex, earthy flavours. It's a completely different experience from light fruity sake — more savoury and textured."
Koshu 古酒 — Aged Sake
MeaningSake aged for an extended period — typically 3 years or more. Very rare. Goes against the usual "fresh is best" sake ethos.
FlavourDeep amber colour. Sherry-like, caramel, dried fruit, honey, soy, mushroom, nutty. Like an oxidised wine or old Madeira.
ServeRoom temp or slightly warm. Small portions — very intense.
💬 How to sell it: "Aged sake is very rare and unique — it develops deep caramel and nutty flavours over years, almost like a sherry or aged rum. Completely different from fresh sake. A real conversation piece for adventurous drinkers."
Sparkling Sake 発泡性
MeaningSake with carbonation — either from in-bottle secondary fermentation (like Champagne) or CO2 injection. Growing category.
FlavourLight, refreshing, often slightly sweet. Fruity, gentle rice flavour, fine bubbles. Very approachable entry point.
ServeWell chilled in a flute or white wine glass. Perfect as an aperitif.
💬 How to sell it: "Sparkling sake is like Champagne's Japanese cousin — light, bubbly, and great as an aperitif or celebration drink. Very easy to enjoy even for people who have never had sake before."
Taruzake 樽酒 — Cedar-aged
MeaningSake aged or stored in Japanese cedar (sugi) barrels. Traditional style, often associated with celebrations and brewery openings.
FlavourDistinctive woody, cedar, pine, almost herbal character. Refreshing and aromatic. Polarising — either love it or don't.
ServeSlightly warm or room temp to bring out the wood aroma. Traditional wooden masu cup if available.
💬 How to sell it: "Taruzake has been aged in cedar wood barrels which gives it a unique woody, pine-like aroma. It's a very traditional style — Japanese breweries often serve it at festivals and celebrations. A real talking point!"

Rice Polishing Ratio (RPR) — Seimaibuai 精米歩合
What % of the rice grain remains after polishing. The outer layer of rice contains proteins and fats that can make sake harsh — polishing removes them.

Lower % = more polished = cleaner, more aromatic sake.
70% = Honjozo/Junmai minimum
60% = Ginjo minimum
50% = Daiginjo minimum
23% = Most extreme polish (ultra-premium)

Think of it like this: the lower the RPR number, the more premium the sake.
Sake Meter Value (SMV) — Nihonshu-do 日本酒度
A scale of sweetness vs dryness.

Positive (+) = Dry — more alcohol than sugar
Negative (−) = Sweet — more residual sugar
Zero (0) = Neutral

Typical ranges:
−3 and below = noticeably sweet
−2 to +1 = slightly sweet to neutral
+2 to +4 = slightly dry (most common)
+5 and above = dry to very dry

Quick tip: Most customers enjoy +1 to +3. Very dry (+6 and up) suits experienced sake drinkers.
Acidity — Sando 酸度
The level of organic acids in the sake. Works alongside SMV to define the overall taste impression.

Average acidity: 1.2–1.5

High acidity (1.6+) = crisp, refreshing, food-friendly. Can make even sweet sake taste drier.
Low acidity (1.0−) = soft, smooth, gentle

Quick tip: High acid + high SMV = sharp and dry. High acid + low SMV = tart-sweet like yoghurt. Low acid + low SMV = soft and sweet.
Alcohol — Arukoru-do アルコール度
Most sake is 14–16% ABV — similar to a full-bodied wine.

Genshu (undiluted) = 17–20%
Standard = 14–16%
Low-alcohol styles = 8–13%
Sparkling = 5–8%

Quick tip for customers: "It's similar strength to wine — about 15%." This reassures people who think sake is very strong (confusing it with shochu or spirits).
Rice Type — Sakamai 酒米
Sake rice (sakamai) is different from table rice — larger grains, higher starch content, lower protein.

Yamada Nishiki — King of sake rice. Rich, full, complex. Widely used in premium sake.
Omachi — Heirloom variety. Earthy, mineral, bold. Harder to grow.
Gohyakumangoku — Light, clean, neutral. Common in Ginjo styles.
Miyama Nishiki — Grown in cold climates. Crisp, clean, elegant.

Quick tip: Think of it like grape varieties in wine — same classification, different rice = different flavour.
Yeast — Kobo 酵母
Yeast drives fermentation and shapes the aroma profile of the sake more than almost anything else.

No. 9 — Most widely used. Fruity, elegant, apple and pear aroma. Standard for Ginjo.
No. 7 — Clean, mild, reliable. Good all-rounder.
No. 14 (Kanazawa) — Fragrant, tropical, melon. Common in premium Ginjo.
Uonuma / House yeasts — Brewery-specific, proprietary character.

Quick tip: Most labels don't list the yeast — it's a bonus detail when they do. Yeast No. 9 is behind the "Ginjo aroma" many people love.
Top 4 Sake Rice TypesSakamai
What is itSake rice is specially grown with large, starchy grains designed for brewing. Like grape varieties in wine, different rice = different flavour character.
Tap to learn
Yamada Nishiki Omachi Gohyakumangoku Miyama Nishiki
Yamada Nishiki 山田錦
Nickname"King of Sake Rice" — the most prized and widely used premium sake rice.
RegionPrimarily Hyogo Prefecture. Very specific growing conditions required.
FlavourRich, full, complex and elegant. Deep umami, balanced fruit, long finish. Versatile across all classifications.
Used forPredominantly Daiginjo and premium Junmai styles. The benchmark rice.
💬 Staff tip: "If a sake lists Yamada Nishiki on the label, it's a quality signal — like seeing Grand Cru on a Burgundy. The brewer has chosen the best rice available."
Omachi 雄町
Nickname"Phantom Rice" — ancient heirloom variety, nearly extinct, now revived. Omachi fans are passionate.
RegionOkayama Prefecture. Difficult to grow — tall plants, prone to falling over.
FlavourEarthy, bold, mineral, savoury umami. Deeper and more rustic than Yamada Nishiki. Dry and complex.
Used forJunmai styles, bold Ginjo. Favoured by sake connoisseurs.
💬 Staff tip: "Omachi has a cult following among sake lovers — it makes bold, earthy sake with a lot of character. If someone says they like natural wine or funky flavours, Omachi is a great recommendation."
Gohyakumangoku 五百万石
RegionNiigata Prefecture and across northern Japan. Second most planted sake rice.
FlavourLight, clean, neutral, crisp. Subtle aroma, refreshing, easy-drinking. Less complex than Yamada Nishiki.
Used forGinjo and lighter Junmai styles. The "clean and dry" Niigata style is largely due to this rice.
💬 Staff tip: "Sake made with Gohyakumangoku tends to be clean and light — great for someone who wants something crisp and refreshing rather than bold and complex."
Miyama Nishiki 美山錦
RegionNagano and Tohoku regions. Cold-weather rice — thrives at high altitude and low temperatures.
FlavourCrisp, elegant, clean. Light fruit, floral, mineral, slightly dry. Refined and delicate.
Used forGinjo and premium Junmai styles. Known for producing elegant, aromatic sake in cold-climate breweries.
💬 Staff tip: "Miyama Nishiki tends to produce elegant, delicate sake — well suited for customers who enjoy light, floral whites like Pinot Gris or Sauvignon Blanc."
4 Flavour ProfilesSSI Framework
What is itThe SSI (Sake Service Institute) groups all sake into 4 flavour types. A quick way to guide customers to the right style.

薫酒 Kunshu 爽酒 Soshu 熟酒 Jukushu 醇酒 Junshu
薫酒 Kunshu — Fragrant
CharacterHighly aromatic, fruity, floral. The most fragrant category. Aroma-driven.
AromaApple, pear, melon, banana, white flowers, tropical fruit, lychee.
TasteLight to medium body, clean, smooth, gentle sweetness, elegant finish.
ClassificationGinjo, Daiginjo, Junmai Ginjo, Junmai Daiginjo.
ServeWell chilled (8–12°C). White wine glass or Ginjo glass.
Pairs with
SashimiOystersLight seafoodOn its own
💬 How to sell it: "If you enjoy floral, fruity white wines like Riesling or Sauvignon Blanc, Kunshu sake is your style — aromatic, delicate, and best served cold."
爽酒 Soshu — Light & Clean
CharacterLight, clean, refreshing, subtle. The most approachable and food-friendly category.
AromaMild, subtle. Light rice, faint floral, clean grain. Aroma is not the focus.
TasteLight body, dry, crisp, refreshing. Clean finish. Effortlessly easy to drink.
ClassificationHonjozo, lighter Junmai, some futsushu (table sake).
ServeChilled or room temp. Ochoko or small glass.
Pairs with
TempuraEdamameSushiAlmost anything
💬 How to sell it: "Soshu is the Pinot Grigio of sake — light, clean, goes with everything. If someone wants something easy and refreshing that won't overpower their food, this is it."
熟酒 Jukushu — Rich & Aged
CharacterDeep, complex, aged character. The rarest and most adventurous category. For experienced sake drinkers.
AromaCaramel, soy, dried fruit, honey, mushroom, sherry-like, amber. Dark and complex.
TasteFull body, rich, intense, long finish. Almost wine-like in complexity.
ClassificationKoshu (aged sake), some Yamahai/Kimoto aged styles.
ServeRoom temp or slightly warm. Small pour — very intense.
Pairs with
Foie grasBlue cheeseDark chocolateRich stews
💬 How to sell it: "Jukushu is aged sake — think of it like Madeira or sherry. Deep, complex, and totally unlike fresh sake. For adventurous drinkers who want something truly unique."
醇酒 Junshu — Full & Earthy
CharacterFull-bodied, rich, earthy, savoury. The most food-friendly and umami-forward category. The "red wine" of sake.
AromaRice, earth, grain, subtle fruit, savoury, lactic (Kimoto styles). Understated but deep.
TasteFull body, rich umami, slightly dry, lingering. Warming and satisfying.
ClassificationFull-bodied Junmai, Kimoto, Yamahai styles.
ServeRoom temp to warm (40–45°C). Ochoko or small ceramic cup. Excellent warmed.
Pairs with
Grilled meatsRich stewsAged cheeseMiso dishes
💬 How to sell it: "Junshu is the bold, full-bodied sake style — think of it like a full-bodied red wine. It has earthy, savoury flavours and goes amazingly with rich food. Try it warm in winter — it's completely different from cold sake."
Quick guideKunshu = fragrant & fruity · Soshu = light & clean · Junshu = full & earthy · Jukushu = aged & complex

Koji Making — The Heart of Sake
What is itKoji (Aspergillus oryzae) is a mould grown on steamed rice. It produces enzymes that convert rice starch into fermentable sugars — the essential first step in sake brewing.
Why it mattersKoji is what makes sake unique. Unlike wine (sugar already in grapes) or beer (malting converts starch), sake uses koji to do this conversion simultaneously with fermentation — called multiple parallel fermentation.
Flavour impactKoji produces amino acids (umami), organic acids, and sugars. The quality and proportion of koji rice directly affects the body, sweetness, and complexity of the final sake.
💬 Staff tip: "Sake is the only alcohol that converts starch to sugar and ferments it at the same time — thanks to this mould called koji. It's why sake is so unique and complex compared to other drinks."
3-Stage Brewing — Sandan Jikomi
What is itIngredients are added in 3 separate stages over 4 days to build up the mash gradually.
Why it mattersGradual addition protects the yeast from shock and allows the brewer to control fermentation. All designated sake must use this method.
💬 Staff tip: "Premium sake takes weeks to brew carefully in stages — this is partly why quality sake costs more than people expect."
Yeast Starter — Shubo / Moto
SokujoQuick starter — lactic acid added directly. Most common modern method. Clean, neutral, consistent.
KimotoTraditional — natural lactic acid bacteria, pole-ramming process. Complex, earthy, robust.
YamahaiModified traditional — no pole-ramming but wild lactic bacteria. Rich, complex, gamey.
💬 Staff tip: "Kimoto and Yamahai on the label = more complex, earthy sake. Think natural wine vs conventional — more personality."
Pasteurisation — Hiire 火入れ
StandardHeated twice — once after brewing, once before shipping. Stable at room temp, longer shelf life.
NamaZero pasteurisation — fresh, vibrant, must stay refrigerated at all times.
Nama ChozoPasteurised before shipping only. Slightly fresher than fully pasteurised.
💬 Staff tip: "If the label says Nama, keep it cold. All other sake is fine at room temp until opened, then refrigerate and drink within a few weeks."
Dilution — Kaga Mizu 加水
What is itMost sake is diluted with water after brewing to bring alcohol from ~18–20% down to ~14–16%.
GenshuNo dilution — bottled at full strength (17–20% ABV). Bolder and more concentrated.
WaterSoft water (Fushimi, Niigata) = soft, delicate sake. Hard water (Nada) = bold, dry sake.
💬 Staff tip: "Water is as important to sake as terroir is to wine. Nada (Kobe) sake is bold; Fushimi (Kyoto) sake is soft and elegant."
In shortRice + Koji + Water + Yeast → ferment 3–5 weeks → press → filter → pasteurise → dilute → bottle. The most labour-intensive fermented drink in the world.
